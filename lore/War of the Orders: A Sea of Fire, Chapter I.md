# War of the Orders: A Sea of Fire, Chapter I

* Note the lack of Renn diplomats

## The *Vel Aurvara*

Shafts of late light broke across the southern Murhaelis, stretching wide over indigo swells. Warm southeast trades pushed a Duvvrah envoy vessel up the sea. To the north, the Mistborne Archipelago loomed half-veiled, blue-black silhouettes in procession.

Sevran Kalethor sat cross-legged beneath canvas awning lashed to four poles, shade thin but welcome. It made a small room of wind and light, enough to gather thoughts without cutting him off from the sea. To his right, two aides worked in silence: Lanis recording observations on rag paper, Dorel cleaning pen nibs and checking corks on ink vials.

The Duvvrah vessel moved with patient confidence. Modest, workmanlike, her iron hull plated in pale desert oak. Steam hissed from vents, carrying the scent of coal across the deck. Chains creaked. Rigging sighed as auxiliary sails drank from the trades.

Fittings were plain blackened iron, smoothed by hand and salt. Yet the sails carried mineral hues of canyon and sun: faded sunset red, pale violet, dusty yellow. Inside the deckhouse, oak paneling bore desert motifs in low relief—dunes, birds, riverbeds long dry. Even oil lamps, steel-bodied and practical, were shaded in muted ochres instead of brass. The ship carried machinery of the age, but never its pageantry.

“Currents split to starboard,” Lanis said softly, shading her notebook.

Sevran nodded. The helmsman had already seen it, adjusting the bow. The ship complied without complaint. Not quick, but obedient. Ash-stone plating along the keel bit water with steadiness.

A gull wheeled once above the masthead, cried, and banked below the deck to the right.  It swept over a single charred plank, half-submerged, rocking in the swell. One face slick with brine, the other studded with nails whose hold had outlived their use. It floated close enough to be reached with a boathook. No flag clung to it, no paint, no initials. Only the faint tar-sweet stink of burned pitch and scorched grain.

Sevran’s chest tightened as the plank drifted close. Instinct made him shift forward slightly, hand twitching toward the boathook. Murmur passed the deck and returned thinner to the awning. One guard shifted. Dorel’s pen tapped against a vial.

Sevran raised a hand. The murmur quieted. He watched until the plank dipped and slid down the starboard side into widening sea. “Let it pass,” he said quietly. The words released the crew to face forward again without shame for the dead.

“Reports said the convoy burned at dusk,” Lanis murmured, eyes on her notebook.

“The trades carry more than wind,” Sevran replied. Even a single board could drift from nightmare to negotiation before the Kaethrin had finished their supper.

Sevran closed his eyes for a moment. *The Kaethrin would arrive with crimson coats and marines grinning through their teeth—the Qora’thular, weary, eager, bruised.* Sevran noticed a slight stiffening of his shoulders as the imagined Kaethrin marched through. He adjusted posture, leaning back just enough to signal calm. Dorel’s hand hesitated over her tablet, pen poised as if waiting for a cue.

“Sevran,” Dorel said, “Shall we add a line to the opening remarks? We have the plank, and the wind carried ash.”

Sevran considered. *To mention the plank would be theater—useful at times, melodramatic at others.* He weighed the balance not as judge, but as mason laying stone. “No,” he said. “Not in the first breath. We do not use the dead to bargain.”

“In the second?”

“In the second,” Sevran said, “we will note that trade dies when convoys burn, and treaties rot faster than wood if guests insist on setting harbors alight.”

The ship angled another degree toward that line. In the distance, haze brightened where glass might be catching sun. Sevran made a note. *remember the posture of the room before you hear a word*.

He did not think of victory. He did not think of failure. He thought of the space in which they would all be forced to breathe—the ledgers, the polished floors, the careful light—and let the shape of it settle around him. Ships carried intent, and he would match with his own. He would arrive like a man stepping into water he had already mapped, even if depths yet to come hid rocks no chart could show.

The haze broke. From the heart of the Murhaelis swells rose the *Vel Aurvara*. At first it seemed a bank of black cliffs, jagged and glinting with stray light, but as the prow caught the sun, illusion dissolved into certainty. An iron hull. Immense blackmirror-plates, streaked with bronze-green sheen, surged out of the mist.

The men on Sevran’s vessel fell quiet. Their ship, proud enough among Duvvrah storm-keels, shrank to irrelevance before that shadow. The *Vel Aurvara* glided forward with steadiness that the sea itself looked small. Waves parted without protest.

Lanis drew breath, fingers tightening around her notebook. “It moves less like a ship than a wall.”

Sevran said nothing, but marked the words. *The Renn had intended that thought: to make the world imagine fortresses, not vessels.*

Seven hundred feet of iron and craft stretched across the horizon. Its beam was broad as a flotilla, sides rising in dark planes of blackmirror plates. The prow curved up into a gilded ram, etched with calligraphy. Gold scrollwork ran down its length, bright even in haze, like scripture too large for mortal reading. Dorel let out a low whistle, then caught himself and straightened.

Sevran leaned forward on the rail. He studied not the hull but the way it moved. Paddle-wheels churned with steady rhythm, smoke venting through gilded cowls that glowed red from furnace-heart within. The smell of banquets wafted over. There was grace in its menace. Where his own ship pitched with the swell, the *Vel Aurvara* seemed immune to sea’s disorder. The stern was no simple cabin or quarterdeck but cathedral afloat. Blackmirror glass rose in tiered walls, windows trimmed in brass so each reflected fire from within. The lantern tower, even unlit in daylight, promised visibility for miles when night fell.

“It glows like a shrine,” murmured one deckhand, half-reverent, half-afraid.

Above the bulk loomed six towering masts, sails furled high. Gold-thread trim caught the late light. Though steam drove the vessel, masts rose as ceremony. Every surface spoke of mastery. Blackmirror plates were cut and laid so perfectly that joins vanished until caught by light. Gold inlay of the prow curled like script mid-sentence, suggesting continuations unseen. Even its smoke was tamed: vented not in ugly stacks but gilded cowls, mouths hooded and angled to preserve the ship’s profile.

The *Vel Aurvara* slowed, paddle-wheels easing, until it lay athwart the Duvvrah vessel’s course. It simply waited. Its size made the waiting itself a command. Sevran understood. *This was no welcome. They had been summoned: to climb their gangways, pass through guard-lined corridors, sit beneath their lantern tower. To refuse was insult. To accept was consent to their ledger.* 

Sevran realized he was still leaned over. He straightened his posture and released the railing. Heat from reflected sun had started to burn his palms.

The Renn flag broke from the midmast: two green laurelfin inside decorative gold circle, on deep blue background. It did not wave like cloth but held taut as though the very air obeyed Renn order.

A longboat dropped from davits, rowed with unison so precise it seemed mechanical. Eight guards in deep blue coats sat stiff, brass trim catching late light. At the stern, a petty officer lifted speaking-trumpet.

“Envoy Sevran,” the voice carried, oddly resonant, “the Ledger-Hall receives you. You and chosen company will attend.”

Sevran nodded his head, though his men muttered. He gave orders quietly, choosing six to accompany him: Dorel, Lanis, and four guards. Fewer would look weak; more would seem challenge. The balance itself was part of the negotiation.

The row across was short but heavy. The *Vel Aurvara* loomed with each stroke, blackmirror hull climbing from sea into sky. The gilded prow curled above like a god’s finger, pointing judgment.

Closer, Sevran could see seams of plating, each rivet hammered like beadwork. The Duvvrah storm-keels, stout as they were, looked crude by comparison: iron patched over wood, practical but exposed.

Their boat approached the boarding-ladder. The Renn had lowered gilded stair, chains tight, unmoving despite the swell. Guards waited at the top, still as carvings. Sevran ascended slowly, every footfall measured. Behind him, his men breathed shallow, as if the very ladder might tally their steps.

At the rail, a Renn officer bowed, hand to brass-trimmed chest. “By the ledger of Renn, your presence is entered.”

Sevran nodded then tilted his head, signaling his six companions to move. One by one, they stepped onto the gangway, the polished iron beneath their boots echoing softly with each measured pace. Guards flanked them at the top, statuesque, hands resting on hilts but unyielding in stillness. Lanis’s eyes flicked to each guard as they rowed, noting posture and grip. She pressed her back straight, suppressing a shiver as the gangway swung slightly in the swell. 

Sevran’s eyes swept the reception hall beyond, taking in the space before his feet touched the floor. He led his party forward with deliberate calm, aware that the act of entering was itself a first statement of intention.

As Sevran stepped inside, the air pressed cool against his skin. Its floor was blackmirror tiles, reflecting soft lantern glow. The scent of polished stone lingered in the air. Brass sconces mounted along the walls held oil lamps, each enclosed in latticed bronze to prevent glare. The ceiling arched low, following the curve of the deck above. Between the beams, narrow skylights ran the length of the hall, allowing shafts of light to play across the floor.

Qora’thular envoys rose to greet them, their presence uneasy yet determined. Six of them stood in green and violet trimmed coats. The weight of loss hung about them like smoke. Their leader, Envoy Sulnora, turned as Sevran approached. His face bore grayness of someone who had stared too long at loss and found no shape in it. Still, he raised his hand in formal greeting, though his fingers trembled with restraint.

“Sevran,” he said. “It’s a comfort that the Duvvrah have joined us in this hour.”

Sevran briefly lowered his head with respect. “We all share these waters. The Kaethrin must remember this.”

They both paused as the Ylvath’mul delegation entered swiftly from across the hall, as if they had been waiting. Their coats were layered in green and ochres. The air changed with them—loamy and thick with musk of rain-rotted wood and fermentation. The Duvvrah guards at Sevran’s back shifted uncomfortably. Three Velut’mora envoys followed closely behind, their light blue coats faintly shimmered in the sconce-light. Along their arms and shoulders rose natural shell-like ridges, pale fungal growth hardened into living ornament, a signature of the Mistborne Archipelago.

The Qora’thular envoys bowed, visible relief passing through their shoulders.

A subtle tension filled the reception hall as delegations took each other's measure. Sevran watched the interplay of glances, the careful positioning of bodies, the weight of unspoken alliances bound by history.

Sulnora moved toward a Ylvath'mul envoy, a tall woman with intricate braids woven through her hair. As he approached, her expression softened with genuine concern. "Lysara. The grain ships you promised—when will they reach our ports?"

Lysara stepped closer, her hand briefly touching his shoulder in a gesture of reassurance. "Three days, old friend. Our barges carry what they can, though the Kaethrin grow bolder each week."

"It will not be enough," Sulnora said quietly. "Our people grow desperate. Some speak of surrender."

"The earth provides what it can," Lysara replied. "But we must look beyond grain. Cython Lyris offers sanctuary to any who would accept it. Your people could find refuge there."

A younger Qora'thular delegate shifted. "To abandon our cities? Our ports?"

Lanis looked away, briefly. Lysara's eyes flashed. "Better abandoned cities than burned ones. The Kaethrin show no mercy to those who resist."

Sevran stepped closer, reading the bond between envoys.

Lysara turned toward him, her gaze measuring. "The Ylvath and Qora'thular have been friends since before the Kaethrin learned to sail, Envoy Sevran. We do not abandon our allies to satisfy ledgers."

A Velut'mora envoy drifted closer, speaking low. "Old bonds grow stronger when tested by fire. But even the strongest rope frays under too much strain."

Sevran studied the pale envoy's face, remembering the charred plank that had drifted past his ship. *The convoy had burned in Velut'mora waters—their waters. And the Renn wish to posture the Murhaelis as their domain.*

Sulnora's jaw tightened. "We ask no more than we can repay. The Kaethrin grow stronger with each victory. If we fall—"

"You will not fall," Lysara said firmly. "Not while the Ylvath stand."

Lanis’s drew a sharp breath, catching Sevran’s attention. “The chamber acoustics,” she whispered, “they’re designed so we can’t speak without the Renn scribes hearing.

"The Kaethrin are not the only threat you face," Sevran interjected, his gaze flicking toward the Renn scribes in their alcoves. "Some predators dress their hunting as hospitality."

Lysara nodded grimly. "The Renn ledgers record more than words. Every conversation here becomes currency in their hands."

A Renn officer approached with measured steps, his brass-trimmed coat catching the lantern light. "Honored envoys," he said, voice carrying formal courtesy, "your quarters have been prepared. The Ledger-Hall will convene at the morning watch."

The reception dissolved into polite farewells as delegations were separated into their escorts. Sevran caught Lysara's eye one final time—she gave a slight nod acknowledging shared understandings before Renn guards guided each party toward different passages.

Sevran signaled his companions to fall behind him. The Renn scribes and attendants watched their passage, eyes flicking as if weighing the weight of each movement. Every gesture, every careful step, seemed magnified in the polished hall.

They turned toward the corridor leading deeper into the *Vel Aurvara*, the sconce-lit passage drawing them away from the reception’s soft heat and measured tension. The party moved with quiet precision, escorted by several Renn.

The space narrowed. Blackmirror walls became gently concave, etched with more calligraphy. Their footsteps echoed differently here: as Sevran’s boots struck, the echo doubled back on itself a half-beat later. He slowed, testing it with a second step. The reply came again. Lanis caught his glance, unease in his jaw before he forced it still.

Sevran noted: *By the time formal negotiations began, the Renn would know which envoys favored their left foot, which cleared their throats when nervous, who unconsciously touched weapon hilts in unfamiliar spaces.*

Their path wound past other delegations’ quarters, and Sevran read the Renn’s subtle hierarchy in every detail. Through an open doorway, he glimpsed the Qora’thular chambers: cramped, positioned directly above the engine rooms where the constant thrum of machinery would prevent restful sleep. The message was clear—supplicants received functional shelter, nothing more.

The Ylvath’mul, by contrast, had been given a spacious suite near the ship’s center, away from noise and vibration. Their doorway was framed in polished brass. 

Most telling were the Velut’mora quarters: small but positioned adjacent to the ship’s archives. The placement whispered its meaning: they were here as witnesses to be noted, not as powers to be feared.

Sevran filed the observations away. Light flooded through tiers of brass-framed windows, scattering across tiles polished to liquid shine. The chamber dwarfed any court Sevran had known: ceilings arched high, chandelier looming above.

Sevran and his company stepped into their envoy chambers. They were no larger than a ship’s officer’s berth, but the Renn had disguised confinement with polish. Ornate mirrors decorated the walls, catching lamplight so the suite seemed deeper than it was. Maritime murals curved overhead—waves painted in lacquer, winds etched in gold, ships rendered in unnerving detail.

Dorel paced by the wall murals, his reflection catching and breaking in the mirrors. “The Qora’thular bleed out in front of us. The Ylvath’mul are already moving grain ships. Where are we?”

He stopped before the mural of a Renn vessel carving through painted seas. It mocked him. His fists clenched. “If we are not with them, we are against ourselves.”

“We could escort their convoys.” Dorel’s voice carried hope that didn’t match his expression. “Our gunners are good, our hulls strong. But…”

Sevran let him go on, eyes steady on the mural above. *How easily we reduce warfare to ledger entries.* Behind Dorel’s suggestions lay burning homes and dying men.

“Then our words must carry the weight our ships cannot. We will offer material aid in measured amounts: Duvvrah escorts, fortified passages, and oversight of relief barges. In negotiations, we will advocate for the Qora’thular and Ylvath’mul where our presence can shift decisions.”

“The reports from Othamarel—” Lanis began.
“I read them.” Sevran said.
“Then you know we can’t just watch this happen,” Dorel cut in.

Lanis continued. “Three fronts—north, flanks, and coast.” Her pen tapping against an ink vial. “We can’t just watch from safety. The Qora’thular don’t have time.”

*watch from safety* The words echoed the charred plank from this morning, the desperate hunger in Sulnora’s eyes, Lysara’s fierce loyalty to a dying ally. Sevran had spent the voyage convincing himself that measured neutrality served the Duvvrah best—that wisdom lay in avoiding broader escalation. But Lanis was right: there would be no safety in a world where the Kaethrin went unopposed.

Sevran nodded, hand resting lightly on the desk. “Then we might shape both instrument and presence. We cannot offer the impossible, but we can advocate what the Duvvrah can do without overextension: targeted support, advice, strategic positioning. We provide what will hold until allies act and Kaethrin forces are balanced by deterrent.”

He straightened, letting the words hang between them. “In the Ledger-Hall, we will be measured not by rhetoric alone, but by how our actions and presence shape outcomes. Our culture prizes endurance, measured craft, and honesty in capability. That is our language before any word is spoken. The Kaethrin may test us; the Renn will observe. Both will judge by the sum of what we bring and the restraint with which we wield it.”

Dorel nodded slowly. “So we help, but not too much. Make the Kaethrin think they have more enemies without getting trapped.”

Sevran’s gaze swept the chamber, pausing on the tense hands of his guards. “Exactly. The Renn provide stage and audience; we provide clarity, precision, and credibility.”

For a moment, silence fell over the chamber. Only the faint hum of engines below vibrated through the floor, steady as a heartbeat. Sevran let the silence linger, eyes drifting over the murals. The Duvvrah valued craft and endurance above flamboyance, and here was the counterpoint: mastery displayed as intimidation, elegance as argument.

One by one, the Duvvrah rose. They filed into the corridor under Renn eyes, each step measured, each breath drawn under scrutiny. Their chamber, though not prison, had been no sanctuary either.

Sevran lingered behind, offering no explanation. When at last he passed the threshold, the others had already dispersed to their appointed berths. Guards led him along the corridors until he reached the small suite marked for him alone. The door closed behind with a quiet finality.

The murmur of distant voices and rhythm of boots on corridor planks had faded with the evening watch. Beyond his door, Renn guards stood their statuesque vigil; within, only hush of oil lamps and faint thrum of ship’s engines remained. Sevran let the silence linger, heavy and instructive, before seating himself at lacquered desk.

Its polished surface reflected his face dimly, wavering outline in lamplight. He pulled out a journal and turned his thoughts to the first delegation he had met—the Qora’thular. Their faces were grave masks worn over hollowing loss. He had seen the wear in their eyes: cities burned, convoys cut down, the wheat and rye that should have fed their people turned instead to ash and hunger.

*They arrive as wounded, not envoys.*

Their grief was sincere, but sincerity was brittle without strength behind it. They leaned already on Ylvath’mul presence, their desperation naked. To speak with them was to touch raw flame; sympathy was inevitable, but trust was dangerous.

*Tragic,* Sevran inked, *but tragedy has no weight in a ledger. Only balance. Only force. They cannot alter sums alone.*

His pen paused. For a moment, he thought of the single charred plank that had drifted past his ship days earlier, memory of a convoy broken. That image belonged as much to the Qora’thular as to himself.

He leaned back from the desk, resting his pen on an inkstand. His eyes fell again on the murals above—the Renn ships cresting endless waves. They seemed to move in the lamplight, advancing as though to swallow his chamber whole.

Rising, he crossed to the porthole. The glass was cold beneath his fingertips. Outside, the sea lay dark, swallowing all color beneath the Beacon-Hall’s glow. Its lights cut sharp across the waves. Sevran watched it vanish into the haze, his own reflection ghosting faintly in the glass.
