# War of the Orders: A Sea of Fire, Prologue

Captain Tulas paced the quarterdeck of the *Ironward*, boots striking wood worn smooth by years of service. The fading sun sagged low over the Murhaelis Sea. Six grain-ships rode heavy in the water under his guard, their holds swollen with wheat and rye bound for Qora’thulas cities tightening under Kaethrin advances. Ceasefire talks loomed in the coming weeks, both sides knowing any agreement would not last. His frigate led three other escorts, bristling with outdated cannons. The guns were iron and reliable, but of an older age. He knew it. His crew knew it. Still, they were here, watchful and grim.

On deck, the rhythms of sea-life played out as they had every dusk. Sailors scrubbed salt 
from the rails, tightened lines, and cursed the endless hauls westward. Steam rose from the engine funnels, mixing coal smoke with salt air, while canvas strained above in the evening breeze. A few men sat cross-legged, mending nets more from habit than use, while others leaned on the gunwales, watching the gulls wheel and shriek above. Tulas allowed himself to believe the voyage might slip into night without incident.

Yet as routine settled into stillness, hints of unease began. The gulls vanished. One moment their cries echoed across the convoy, the next they were gone, their absence sudden enough to be remarked on by deckhands. The wind soured, carrying something acrid across the decks. Sailors muttered among themselves. “Bad wind.” No storm clouds showed, no swell rose, yet unease spread in low voices from ship to ship.

A glance from Tulas was enough. His crew had sailed under him long enough to read the slight shake of his head: *hold steady*. Deckhands returned to their tasks, their movements deliberate. But respect flowed both ways—these men deserved honesty, not false reassurance. Tulas let his eyes drift to the southern haze, acknowledging what they all felt: the pressing silence, the sea’s unnatural stillness that set every veteran instinct on edge.

The men worked faster than need demanded. Voices turned sharp. Older crewmen spat into the sea, another sailor swore and went below for his flask. Tulas tasted the air again, ash clinging at the back of the throat like soot. He remembered harbors drowned in smoke, pyres lit on the waves themselves.

“The ash has caught us.”

A scarred veteran on the foredeck gave the words flatly, staring southward. They fell like a dropped anchor. Silence spread, then a ripple of curses. *The ash has caught us*—sailors’ shorthand for the Kaethrin fleet. Tulas’s stomach turned.

Tulas shouted. “Kaethrin on the south!”

Sailors scrambled to the guns, hands fumbling in the fading light. Lanterns swung wild as captains barked for order, their voices cracking with fear they tried to hide. On the escorts, signal flags jerked up and down, frantic calls for readiness flashing in the last of the daylight.

The cargo crews were already unraveling. Men shouted over one another, stumbling through half-drilled routines. Some froze outright, staring wide-eyed at the horizon. They knew the stories: Kaethrin fleets that seemed to rise from nowhere, plumes of fire across the water, ships burning before a shot could be fired in reply.

Hulls emerged in the haze, Tulas’s gaze fixed on the horizon: six, then eight, then more closing in. “Ready yourselves!” 

One by one, the Kaethrin ships bared themselves. Along the obsidian-gilded plating of their hulls, carvings once hidden in shadow began to gleam red. First a few lines, then whole seams lit up, crawling across the vessels in pulsing, angular patterns.

“Guns—get the guns!” Tulas bellowed. Wheels screeched as the old cannons were swung about, crews coughing in the sulfur haze as they readied shells with shaking hands.

The Ashrunners struck first. They came in fast on the flanks, furnace-lances brimming with heat. Their flames cut across the water, punching through escort hulls, melting timbers, and blistering iron. The ships reeled, crews screaming as decks buckled and plating sagged. The *Ironward* fired a ragged volley in return, but the skimmers veered aside like wolves, circling back to strike again.

Tulas seized his moment as one Ashrunner swept too close, its pilot believing him to be easy prey. “Port guns—lead the bastard!” The frigate’s old cannons swung with grinding precision, crews sighting down iron barrels as the skimmer banked toward another grain-ship. The volley cracked like thunder. Shells screamed across the gap, catching the Kaethrin vessel as its furnace-lance ignited. The impact shattered obsidian plating,  rupturing something vital within. The skimmer convulsed, flame guttering to steam, then listed hard as seawater poured through its broken hull. The *Ironward*’s crew loosed a ragged cheer.

Broadsides loomed in the rear, launching molten shells. Shards of slag hissed through rigging, slicing ropes and setting sails aflame. One shell struck an escort directly, collapsing a whole section of deck into glowing ruin. The bombardment grew wilder as assault continued. A cannon aimed for the *Ironward* shot high, the shell sailing over her stern to strike one of the grain-ships beyond. The impact punched through its wooden hull, igniting the wheat stores within. Flame raced through the hold, and the grain-ship erupted in a tower of fire and burning chaff. The explosion lit the convoy in hellish orange, wheat dust floating across the water.

Boarding ships came next. Low, black-prowed vessels advanced on the cargo under partial sail. From their decks, pneumatic launchers thumped, sending heavy chains arcing through the dimming sky. Links slammed onto grain-ships’ rails and masts with iron finality. Through the smoke, Tulas could see crews scrambling at the chains, but they held fast. Dark shapes massed on the Kaethrin decks.

The first grain-ship fell quickly. Clouds of ash vented over the railing, engulfing those aboard. Its crew, coughing blind, could barely raise arms before Kaethrin steel swept over. Marines leaped across boarding planks, scattering defenders into disarray. Revolvers boomed as axes crashed through barricaded doors. But as the Kaethrin pressed toward the ship’s hold, a dying sailor cut loose a cargo winch. Two tons of grain sacks crashed down, crushing three boarders beneath and buying precious seconds for the survivors to leap overboard. The boarding captain snarled, putting a final bullet in his gut.

Through the smoke and chaos, Tulas watched his convoy die ship by ship. 

The *Ironward* convulsed as another furnace-lance swept across her deck, turning iron railings cherry-red in seconds. Tulas watched a crewman stumble backward, his hands fused to the metal he’d tried to steady himself against. The man’s screams cut through the air as strips of palm-flesh clung to the rail.

“Water!” Tulas shouted, but the deck buckets had already boiled away to steam. The next lance-strike punched through the starboard hull. Scalding air rushed through the ship’s interior, and Tulas heard his men shrieking in the lower decks as the metal bulkheads became furnace walls. The smell hit him: charred wood, melted tar, and skin.

Then the magazine caught. The explosion flung Tulas across the deck. His body struck the railing, folding him in half. 

The rest of the convoy dissolved in terror. Captains shouted to one another, voices muffled in the panic. Lanterns swung wildly, their light swallowed by smoke. One ship cut its anchors and drifted starboard, colliding with another. Crews stumbled blind, vomiting black soot, some leaping into the water only to thrash sightless as oil and splinters burned on the waves.

As the last resistance broke, aftermath slowed into ritual. Ashpriests stepped forward, calling the boarding parties into a low chant. Survivors were dragged to the mainmast, bound where they stood. Each knot tied in intricate patterns to prepare the men for sacrifice. One priest paused at a wounded sailor who’d lost too much blood, felt for a pulse, then shook his head. That one was pushed overboard. Pyres were lit with ceremony: urns cracked, flame poured carefully at their feet. The sailors shrieked as fire climbed their bodies.

The sea itself seemed aflame. Burning debris drifted between the ships, sails collapsing in showers of sparks. Oil and tar floated alight in long crimson trails, the dead and living adrift among them. As silence crept in, the Kaethrin pivoted away in formation, seized grain-ships turning with them. Dusk passed to nightfall over the Murhaelis, pyres billowing over stilling waters. The last groans of the Qora’thulas gave way to silence.
