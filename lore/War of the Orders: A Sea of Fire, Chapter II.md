# War of the Orders: A Sea of Fire, Chapter II

## Lavat Nin: Order of the Ninefold Harmony

*The Harmonist* cut southward with an ease that made the sea seem its accomplice. Swells broke against its basalt hull in hushed rhythms, reluctant to disturb its course. Turbines throbbed beneath the waterline, their pulse carrying farther than sight, unsettling the crews of fishing craft that scattered before its approach.

The Mistborne Archipelago lay on either side. Jagged islets rose from the turquoise shallows like teeth gnawed down to stubs, their flanks dressed in palms and feverish greenery. Local mariners believed the fog carried old spirits, remnants of drowned civilizations. To the crew of *The Harmonist*, the haze was little more than a curtain—something to part. Every passing ripple left the mist in coils behind them, obedient to the vessel’s quiet insistence.

In the chart room, beneath the steady glow of oil lamps, Valsin Drovath sat in a wide oak chair, maritime intelligence spread across the lacquered table in front of him. Trade manifests, patrol reports, port registries—all acquired through careful diplomatic channels and less official means. Moralen Loth stood opposite him, her fingers tracing shipping routes marked in red ink.

“The pattern is unmistakable,” she said, tapping a cluster of notations near Mistborne ports. “Resiluth shipments logged at departure—three hundred tons to Qeth Harbor. Arrival manifest shows one hundred fifty. The difference doesn’t simply evaporate.”

Valsin lifted a port authority ledger, scanning its entries. “Could be measurement error. Different scales, different clerks…”

“Three separate convoys, Valsin. All showing fifty percent losses.” Moralen’s pen clicked against the table. “Either every harbormaster from here to the southern reaches has forgotten basic arithmetic, or someone’s making a considerable profit.”

“Profit’s one thing. But untuned Resiluth should be destroying their equipment within months. Unstable resonance, structural failures—”

“Unless they’re learning to tune it themselves.” Her voice carried an edge that made Valsin look up. “Every ton that reaches unauthorized hands is a potential breakthrough. Today it’s crude weapons that explode after a dozen shots. Tomorrow it could be artillery that doesn’t betray position with muzzle flash.”

Valsin set down the ledger, considering. “You’re assuming competence they may not possess.”

“I’m assuming intelligence we’d be fools to underestimate.” Moralen moved around the table, pointing to shipping lanes marked in different colors. “Look at the raiding patterns. Six months ago, Kaethrin attacks were opportunistic—fishing boats, isolated merchants. Now they’re hitting fortified convoys with precision timing. Someone’s providing them with intelligence, and someone’s providing them with tools.”

“The timing could be coincidence.”

“Three ‘weather losses’ during the calmest season on record?” Her fingers drummed against the chart table in measured rhythm. “Two more convoys taken by pirates who left no survivors, no demands, no witnesses. Either the Murhaelis has developed very selective weather patterns, or we’re dealing with coordinated theft.”

Valsin studied the shipping routes, his diplomatic mind weighing angles. “Even if you’re right about systematic smuggling, confronting the Renn directly risks appearing aggressive rather than collaborative.”

“The Renn already know.” Moralen’s jaw tightened. “Their port inspectors aren’t blind. They’re choosing to look away because disrupting the flow would require actual enforcement, and enforcement costs more than it profits.”

“Then we need them to see the profit in cooperation.” Valsin tapped the chart showing the *Vel Aurvara’s* position. “Present ourselves as solving their problem rather than exposing their failure.”

“Their failure is the problem.” The distinction clearly mattered to her. “A power that lets strategic resources flow to enemies doesn’t deserve the name of governance. If they wanted regional stability, they’d marshal resources for proper oversight.”

“And if we push too hard, they’ll frame us as interlopers seeking to undermine legitimate authority.”

“Legitimate authority enforces its own laws.” Moralen’s pen clicked faster. “We arrive offering partnership—joint patrols, standardized inspection protocols, intelligence sharing to identify smuggling networks before they can establish new routes.”

Valsin moved to the porthole, watching the Mistborne Archipelago drift past. “Partnership implies equality. But what you’re describing sounds more like supervision.”

“I’m describing competence.” Her tone carried growing frustration. “Look at the results: the Qora’thular watch their cities burn while begging for grain shipments. The Ylvath’mul exhaust themselves feeding refugees. Each crisis spawns three others because no one coordinates, no one plans beyond immediate necessity.”

“And the Lavan provide the coordination they lack?”

“The Lavan understand systems.” She returned to the charts, her movements sharp with conviction. “Trade irregularities create military imbalances. Military imbalances invite external aggression. External aggression destroys the commerce that funded the original trade. The cycle is predictable because it’s structural.”

Valsin studied her expression, reading both strategic insight and ideological fervor. “Your confidence in systematic solutions is considerable.”

“My confidence rests on documented patterns.” She gestured toward the scattered intelligence reports. “Every region that lacks proper oversight follows the same sequence: lax enforcement, resource diversion, armed instability, economic collapse. The Renn are halfway through that cycle right now.”

“Yet they might argue their methods have maintained regional stability for decades.”

“Stability?” Moralen’s voice sharpened. “Ships burning in supposedly protected waters? Convoys disappearing from patrolled routes? Their ‘stability’ is an illusion maintained by distance and selective blindness.”

Valsin turned back from the porthole, his expression thoughtful. “Then we frame intervention as burden-sharing rather than criticism. Help them achieve the oversight they claim to want.”

“And when they reveal they don’t actually want oversight?”

“Then they reveal their priorities.” His diplomatic mask slipped slightly, showing calculating ambition beneath. “Reasonable actors choose security over short-term profit. Those who reject collaborative frameworks expose themselves as obstacles to regional peace.”

Moralen secured one of the charts with a brass clip. “The framework is sound, Valsin. Regional instability stems from inadequate oversight of strategic resources. Proper regulation restores balance. Collaborative enforcement prevents exploitation.”

“And Resiluth regulation follows naturally from such comprehensive oversight.”

“Resiluth regulation follows necessarily from any effective security framework.” She rolled up another chart, her movements precise and controlled. “The metal flows where it should, when it should, in quantities that maintain balance rather than arms races. That’s not exploitation—that’s responsible stewardship.”

“The other delegates may view our uninvited arrival less charitably than we intend.”

“The other delegates will judge results, not protocols.” Moralen stacked the intelligence reports with military efficiency. “We arrive with solutions to problems they’ve failed to address. We offer resources they desperately need. If they reject partnership out of wounded pride, the consequences rest with their judgment.”

Valsin watched her organize the documents, noting the satisfaction in her movements. “Your faith in logic overcoming political emotion is admirable.”

“My experience with consequences overrides political emotion.” She met his gaze directly. “Chaos serves no one except opportunists and raiders. Order serves everyone—even those too proud to request it properly.”

The chart room fell quiet except for the steady pulse of engines below. Outside, a sleek courier vessel gained on them, its black hull cutting through the mist with unnatural speed. Resiluth-enhanced engines, Valsin noted—a demonstration of the technological advantage they sought to protect.

“They’re signaling,” he observed, watching the approaching craft.

The *Harmonist* slowed its engines as the courier drew alongside. Lines were thrown, caught, and secured with military precision. From the smaller ship, a figure leapt to the ladder and climbed, coat snapping in the wind.

A young officer saluted as he reached the deck. Valsin and Moralen had emerged from the chart room. His voice carried, clear and trained:

“Envoys of the Harmony, I bear sealed word from Vey Avelar.”

His words tightened the air around them. Valsin dismissed the attending crew with a gesture, then extended his hand. The courier placed a basalt coffer in his grip.

Valsin pulled a small key from his breast pocket. He unlocked the coffer and unfolded its vellum contents within. He read silently, his face a mask.

*Choir of Inarith has risen. Coordinated attack in Maraleth. Port destabilized. Casualties ongoing. Details to follow.*

He handed the note to Moralen. She read twice before lowering the sheet. The name of the city weighed on her—Maraleth, one of the Resiluth-forges of the west, a port where the Lavan navy was sung into being.

“They chose their moment,” she murmured.

“They chose poorly,” Valsin replied, his voice measured but tight.

For an instant neither spoke. Around them, the sea seemed to lean closer, the great quiet between waves pressing against their thoughts. The courier stood at respectful attention, eyes forward.

Valsin returned the coffer. The courier saluted and descended back to his vessel.

Moralen remained at the rail as the courier ship pulled away, its wake already dissolving into the larger pattern of swells. She studied the message again. *Coordinated attack.*

Her fingers found their familiar rhythm against the rail, tapping in measured time. The timing troubled her more than the uprising itself. Maraleth was a strategic target, chosen for maximum disruption while their focus lie southward.

“The Third Harmonic Regiment is stationed two days south of Maraleth,” she said without turning. “Swift deployment should contain this before it spreads.”

Valsin joined her at the rail. “Containment assumes it hasn’t spread already.”

“It won’t.” Her tone carried professional certainty. “The Choir operates in isolated cells. Cut the head, the body stops moving. Standard insurgency tactics—effective in the short term, vulnerable to systematic response.”

She folded the message, tucking it inside her coat. “This actually strengthens our position here. The other Orders see Lavan forces engaged domestically, they might assume we’re distracted. When we demonstrate otherwise—when we handle both situations simultaneously—it reinforces our capabilities.”

“You sound almost pleased by the timing.”

Moralen considered this. “Challenges reveal competence. The Choir thinks they’ve chosen a moment of weakness. They’ll discover their error soon enough.” Her fingers continued their steady rhythm. “Meanwhile, we proceed as planned. The regional situation remains our priority.”

The southern haze began to thin, a massive shape emerging on the horizon. The *Vel Aurvara* materialized like a steel cliff rising from the sea, its blackmirror hull catching fragments of light.

Moralen straightened, her attention shifting from domestic concerns to the diplomatic theater ahead. “The Renn have prepared quite a stage for us.”

“Stage management has always been their strength,” Valsin observed.

“Strength?” She studied the approaching vessel’s imposing silhouette. “It’s theater. Impressive at distance, but theater nonetheless.” Her voice carried a note of professional assessment rather than dismissal. “Still, we should respect their preparation. They’re not hosting this summit accidentally.”

The rebellion in Maraleth grew distant, reduced to a tactical problem to be resolved by subordinates while she focused on larger strategic concerns. The real work—the kind that shaped regions rather than merely suppressed discord—waited ahead.

The boatswain’s call rang sharp as the *Vel Aurvar* assumed it’s full scale, its hull blotting out the sky. Valsin leaned forward at the rail, the gleam of appreciation in his eyes. 

A skiff detached from the *Vel Aurvara’s* side. Eight oarsmen in deep blue coats rowed in perfect unison, their brass-trimmed uniforms catching the late light. At the stern, a Renn officer raised his speaking trumpet as they approached the *Harmonist*.

“Envoys of the Lavan Order,” the voice carried with formal courtesy, “the Ledger-Hall receives you at your convenience. You and your chosen retinue will be our honored guests.”

Valsin selected six to accompany them: Moralen, two aides, and three guards. Fewer would appear weak; more might seem provocative. 

Their skiff struck rhythm against the turquoise swells, each motion cutting them closer. Valsin murmured something about the size of the engines that could move this steel cathedral. Moralen allowed him his marvel, but she wondered if it was Resiluth behind the spectacle.

The boarding stair was lowered, a ladder wrought of brass and gold-chased chain. It gleamed in the sun, unmoving against the swell, an illusion of perfect stillness. 

They rose, deliberately slow. At the top, the guards stood in ranks. Their coats dyed blue and trimmed in brass, still as idols.

An officer bowed at their arrival, announcing their entry into the ledger. Valsin inclined his head graciously. Moralen said nothing. 

They crossed the gangway, the iron beneath their boots singing in faint echoes. She let her gaze sweep the entryway. Others might see majesty; she saw a theater. Every detail was script, every surface staged for awe.

The Lavan stepped through tense silence in the reception hall, blackmirror tiles stretching between three Renn envoys and themselves. Brass sconces cast light through latticed bronze enclosures, painting long, sharp shadows across the arched ceiling. Every detail seemed magnified, emphasizing the smallness of the assembly compared to the space around them.

Jorath, the tallest among them, stepped forward. “Welcome,” a hint of unease threaded his voice. “Your arrival was… unexpected.”

“The regional situation has developed certain complexities that merit broader consultation,” Valsin replied, his voice carrying across the hall with measured authority. “We trust our perspective may prove… constructive to these deliberations.”

He paused, allowing the weight of diplomatic courtesy to settle. “Recent developments suggest that current oversight frameworks may require additional resources to achieve their intended effectiveness.”

“We maintain the region as best we can,” Jorath replied, an edge creeping into his voice despite diplomatic protocol. “Trade flows under our supervision. All remains within established legal parameters.”

Valsin’s expression remained perfectly cordial. “Naturally. Yet we’ve observed certain… irregularities that suggest the scope of challenges may exceed what any single authority could reasonably manage alone.” His tone carried sympathy. “The Resiluth trade, for instance, appears to encounter frequent complications between ports of departure and arrival.”

Beside Jorath, Maelis spoke. “All maritime trade faces inherent risks—”

“Of course,” Valsin acknowledged smoothly. “Though when such risks follow consistent patterns, one wonders whether collaborative approaches might prove more effective .” He clasped his hands behind his back. “The Lavan have considerable experience with complex logistics networks. Perhaps our resources could complement existing efforts.”

Jorath’s jaw tightened slightly. “Are you suggesting our current methods are inadequate?”

“I’m suggesting that no single Order possesses unlimited capacity. The Kaethrin situation alone would strain any administrative framework. When combined with the challenges of monitoring extensive trade networks…” He let the implication hang delicately in the air.

“The Kaethrin are indeed a concern,” Maelis said, her voice carrying more authority now. “Which is precisely why we’ve convened these discussions.”

“And yet the Kaethrin attacks correlate remarkably with regions where trade oversight faces resource constraints,” Moralen observed, speaking for the first time. Her tone remained neutral. “Intelligence suggests their tactical capabilities have improved substantially. One might wonder about their supply sources.”

The third Renn envoy, who had remained silent, stepped forward. “Are you implying a connection between legitimate commerce and hostile activities?”

“We’re noting patterns that merit investigation,” Valsin replied smoothly. “The Lavan have observed similar dynamics in other regions. Unregulated resource flows often create unintended strategic vulnerabilities.” He smiled with diplomatic warmth. “This is precisely the sort of challenge where collaborative oversight proves invaluable.”

Jorath’s lips pressed into a thin line. “And this… collaborative oversight would take what form?”

“Joint patrols, perhaps. Standardized inspection protocols to ensure consistency across ports. Intelligence sharing to identify potential security risks before they develop.” Valsin’s tone suggested these were merely preliminary thoughts. “Nothing that would supersede existing authority, naturally. Simply additional resources to strengthen current frameworks.”

“You’re proposing to station Lavan forces in Renn waters?” Maelis asked, her diplomatic mask slipping slightly.

“We’re offering to share the burden of regional security,” Moralen replied with courtesy. “The current situation clearly exceeds what any single Order should be expected to manage independently. Collaborative response seems prudent.”

The hall grew quiet. The mirrored floor reflected each diplomat’s carefully controlled posture, revealing tension beneath diplomatic courtesy.

Jorath straightened, reasserting formal protocol. “These are complex matters requiring considerable deliberation. Perhaps we should adjourn to allow proper consultation with our respective authorities.”

“Naturally,” Valsin agreed with gracious understanding. “Though I trust we can all agree that expedited resolution serves everyone’s interests. The regional situation continues to develop while we deliberate.”

The third Renn stepped forward, his voice tight but formally correct. “Envoy Valsin, Attaché Moralen, we have prepared quarters for visiting delegations. If you would permit us to show you to your accommodations?”

The Renn officer gestured toward a passage lined with polished brass fixtures. “This way, if you please.” Guards flanked them at respectful distance—not quite escort, not quite honor guard. The message was subtle but clear: guests moved where permitted, when permitted.

- - -

Their path wound through corridors that demonstrated Renn priorities with calculated precision. They passed the Qora’thular quarters first—cramped chambers positioned directly above the engine rooms where constant mechanical thrum would prevent restful sleep. The placement spoke louder than words: supplicants received functional shelter, nothing more. The Ylvath’mul had been granted more spacious accommodations near the ship’s stable center, their doorway framed in polished brass—the courtesy shown to valued but junior partners.

- - -

The Lavan suite lay in the ship’s diplomatic quarter, neither the finest nor the poorest placement. The chambers were well-appointed but clearly prepared hastily—fresh paint couldn’t quite mask the scent of recent construction, and the furniture bore the uniform quality of items selected for adequacy rather than impression. “Quarters suited to your delegation’s… particular requirements,” the Renn officer said with diplomatic care. “We regret the modest appointments. The Renn do not typically accommodate uninvited dignitaries.” 

The Renn officer bowed precisely and withdrew, leaving the Lavan delegation alone. The door closed with a soft but definitive click—solid enough to muffle conversation, light enough to avoid seeming like imprisonment.

Valsin moved to the center of the chamber, his diplomatic posture relaxing into something more natural. “Well-appointed prison,” he observed, running his fingers along the lacquered desk. “Notice how the mirror placement lets them observe our movements from the corridor without seeming intrusive.”

Moralen examined the maritime murals curving overhead—waves painted in lacquer, winds etched in gold, ships rendered in unnerving detail. “Theater, as expected. Though the workmanship is competent.” She tapped the wall experimentally. “Sound-dampened. They don’t want our conversations carrying, but they also don’t want to appear to be eavesdropping.”

“The furniture tells the real story.” Valsin settled into one of the chairs, testing its construction. “Uniform quality, recently acquired. They scrambled to accommodate us.” His mouth curved slightly. “For all their preparation, they weren’t prepared for uninvited guests.”

Moralen paused before a porthole, studying the view of other delegations’ quarters. “The Qora’thular placement was deliberate humiliation. Engine noise, heat, constant vibration. But our accommodations…” She turned back to survey the room. “Neither honored guest nor dismissed supplicant. They’re still calculating how to treat us.”

“Which gives us room to maneuver.” Valsin leaned back, his formal bearing dissolving entirely now that they were alone. “Did you catch Jorath’s reaction when you mentioned supply sources? His jaw went tight.”

“They know about the Resiluth flow. The question is whether they’re complicit or simply overwhelmed.” Moralen moved away from the porthole, her voice dropping to conversational tones. “Either way, we’ve planted the right seeds.“​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

“We have shifted the axis,” Valsin said at last. “The Renn will recover, yes—they are practiced at seeming immovable. But the first impression will remain.”

“What matters now is not to overreach. We must give them no foothold. We have shown inevitability; now we must sustain it.”

She studied him for a long moment. To Valsin, inevitability was a mask worn perfectly. She admired it. Yet beneath the same logic she heard the deeper current: the shape of order that demanded not merely acceptance, but reverence.

“And when the delegations begin?” she asked.

“Then we play the longer line.” Valsin paced a step, clasping one hand over the other. “Force the Renn to police Resiluth—if not openly, then in effect. Bind them to the task they denied. If they resist, their weakness shows. If they comply, we set the terms. Either outcome feeds us.”

Moralen smiled faintly. “And the others?”

“They will posture. They will angle for concessions. But they will not forget who broke the Renn’s composure.”

Valsin dismissed himself with a nod, already turning toward his notes for the sessions ahead.

Moralen remained seated after he left, the chamber’s hush pressing close. She exhaled slowly, letting the mask of composure slip by a measure.

The initiative was theirs. For now. She did not delude herself: the Renn would craft a counterweight. Balance never held for long. Yet in that flux lay proof—the world demanded measure, demanded the hand that could set disorder into line.

Conflict was not misfortune. It was symphony. Each Order an instrument, rough or refined, some swelling, some faltering. Most deaf to the music they themselves produced. She could already hear the phrases building, the hidden structure waiting to be drawn out.

The Renn had tried to hold the melody; Valsin had answered. She would be the one to bind them all together, to take their clashing notes and hammer them into something whole.

Her eyes closed, not in weariness but in anticipation. In her mind, the hall stretched before her once more, the delegations arrayed like an orchestra at rest. And she did not doubt, when the time came, they would play as the Lavan willed.
